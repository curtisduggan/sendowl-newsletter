🎤 Dani on the Pod
"The art matters."

I sat down with writer David McIlroy on his podcast recently. We talked about Brandon Sanderson's keynote "You Are the Art," and why it stuck with me. His argument: creation isn't about consumption. The point of making something is to reflect on who you are, to change, to get better. The product is a byproduct of that process.

I think the creator economy is in an unhealthy moment right now. There's real pressure to produce volume. AI can generate passable content. And there are people telling creators they essentially won't matter in a few years because something else can create what they do. That framing reduces all of creation down to output for someone else to consume. It misses the point entirely.

The people who are going to come out on top of this are the people who paid attention to the craft, who ignored some of the hype, who kept making things that required them to exist. Remove the creator from the product and if what's left isn't worth buying, you're on the right track.

We also talked about Wattpad, Silicon Valley's complicated relationship with art, and why I once motorcycled through a Peruvian protest zone. It got weird. David's a fantasy writer himself, so the conversation went places.

🔍 The Deep Dive
Your ebook has a ChatGPT problem

A basic how-to PDF used to take a weekend to write. Now a chatbot can produce one in about 20 minutes. The advice in it won't be bad. But the same advice is available to anyone for free, customized in real time.

If your product is mostly information that a chatbot could also provide, your customer has a free alternative that didn't exist two years ago. That's worth sitting with for a minute.

So what are the sellers who are growing doing differently?

Julien Tauban at Loops de la Crème records, photographs, programs, and tests every virtual instrument himself. His products from 2014 still generate sales. When any producer can generate a sample pack with AI tools, the one built by someone who spent months getting the sound right becomes more interesting. The value was never the samples. It was Julien's ear.

Sara Borgstede's simple 3-day meal plan outsells her comprehensive programs. Not because simplicity is inherently better, but because her audience trusts her judgment about what to leave out. An AI can simplify information too. The difference is that Sara's readers believe her when she says "ignore the rest, just do these three things." That trust took years to build.

Katie Dunlop at Love Sweat Fitness hosts quarterly Town Halls where her most engaged customers decide what to build next. She's selling access to a community and a decision-making process. You can't get that from a prompt.

The pattern: all three sell something that requires a specific person to exist. Remove Julien, Sara, or Katie from their products, and what's left isn't worth buying.

If your product is an information download with no human fingerprint on it, that's the thing to fix. Add your judgment. Add access to you. Add the context that only comes from doing the work for years. A chatbot can't provide those.
 
🌟 Seller Spotlight
Cataleya & Happy Stitches

Cataleya is 10 years old and she runs a legitimate LLC called Happy Stitches out of her house. She sews bags by hand, sells digital sewing tutorials through SendOwl, and recently took out a loan from her parents to buy a Goldendoodle named Flora.

Her mom Rai set the whole thing up as a real business with real consequences. Happy Stitches has tax obligations and an investment account. The family splits Cataleya's profits into four buckets: 25% for materials, 25% for overhead, and the remaining 50% becomes her income. Half of that income goes into a brokerage account she can't touch until she's 21.

She's currently repaying the puppy loan from her net profits while also covering Flora's monthly expenses. A 10-year-old with a more structured profit allocation system than most adult freelancers.

Rai's philosophy: "When you start a kid business, you should get their business compliant. We teach the wrong aspect from a beginning age if we're not valuing their skill set."

Cataleya is homeschooled, and shipping packages to new states doubles as geography lessons. Her advice for when things get overwhelming: "You just have to take a step back and come back to it." Not bad for a fifth grader managing a puppy loan.

⚡ Quick Wins

Specificity sells.

"A 12-week kettlebell program for busy parents" will outsell "fitness guide" every single time. People search for their exact problem, and they buy the thing that sounds like it was made for them.

Own your list.

Dawn Nicole has 84k on Instagram and 238k on Pinterest. Her advice: no social media platform is as important as your email list. It's the only channel they can't take from you.
