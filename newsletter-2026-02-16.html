🔍 The Deep Dive
AI can write your ebook. Here's why that's good news.

AI can generate a passable ebook in about 20 minutes now. A decent how-to PDF, complete with chapters, formatting, and actionable advice. Two years ago that would have taken a weekend. Five years ago, weeks.

If you sell generic information products, this is uncomfortable. A customer can get 80% of the value of a basic how-to guide by spending an hour with ChatGPT. That's a real problem for anyone whose product is mostly information that a chatbot could also provide.

But here's what I keep seeing from the sellers who are actually growing right now: they're not competing with AI. They're using the space AI created.

The information was never the real value. The creator was.

Creators who've added live components, community access, or personal accountability to their products are pulling ahead. Katie Dunlop at Love Sweat Fitness doesn't just sell workout plans. She hosts quarterly Town Halls where her most engaged customers decide what to build next. Her supplement line nearly sold out the first week because the demand already existed before they launched.

Sara Borgstede discovered that her simple 3-day meal plan outsold her comprehensive programs. Why? Because people are more likely to buy something that feels doable than something that feels comprehensive. AI can't replicate the judgment call of knowing which information to leave out.

Julien Tauban at Loops de la Crème records, photographs, programs, and tests every virtual instrument himself. His products from 2014 still generate sales. When every producer can generate a sample pack with AI, the one that was recorded by a human who spent months getting the sound right becomes more valuable, not less.

AI is a filter. It's raising the floor (anyone can produce decent content) while also raising the ceiling for creators who bring something a model can't: lived experience, taste, community, and accountability.

The practical question for every digital product seller: could someone get 80% of your product's value by spending an hour with ChatGPT? If yes, it's time to add the human elements that make it worth paying for. Personal feedback. Community access. Live interaction. Curation based on real experience.

If you already use AI tools to research and outline your work, you're ahead of most sellers. Use AI for the draft. Show up for the craft.
 
🌟 Seller Spotlight
Cataleya & Happy Stitches

Cataleya is 10 years old and she runs a legitimate LLC called Happy Stitches out of her house. She sews bags by hand, sells digital sewing tutorials through SendOwl, and recently took out a loan from her parents to buy a Goldendoodle named Flora.

Her mom Rai set the whole thing up as a real business with real consequences. Happy Stitches has tax obligations and an investment account. The family splits Cataleya's profits into four buckets: 25% for materials, 25% for overhead, and the remaining 50% becomes her income. Half of that income goes into a brokerage account she can't touch until she's 21.

She's currently repaying the puppy loan from her net profits while also covering Flora's monthly expenses. A 10-year-old with a more structured profit allocation system than most adult freelancers.

Rai's philosophy: "When you start a kid business, you should get their business compliant. We teach the wrong aspect from a beginning age if we're not valuing their skill set."

Cataleya is homeschooled, and shipping packages to new states doubles as geography lessons. Her advice when things get overwhelming: "You just have to take a step back and come back to it."

She is ten.

⚡ Quick Wins

Specificity sells.

"A 12-week kettlebell program for busy parents" will outsell "fitness guide" every single time. People search for their exact problem, and they buy the thing that sounds like it was made for them.

Own your list.

Dawn Nicole has 84k on Instagram and 238k on Pinterest. Her advice: no social media platform is as important as your email list. It's the only channel they can't take from you.

EU VAT applies to you.

17-27% on digital products, depending on the customer's country. Yes, even on a $5 ebook. Yes, even for solo creators. A surprising number of sellers don't find this out until after they start selling internationally.
